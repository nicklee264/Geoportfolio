How to use the map

The map above is completely interactive, the coloured tiles of Melbourne being able to display data when the mouse is hovered over it. The data displayed will indicate the area location name and the value depending on the map selected.

Instructions to using the map:

  • To zoom out: Either press the minus symbol in the top right corner of the map or scroll the mouse wheel downwards
  • To zoom in: Press the plus symbol in the top right corner of the map or scroll the mouse wheel upwards
  • To move around the map: Click and drag the mouse or click on the map and use the arrow keys to move around the map
  • Interacting with the tiles: Hovering the mouse arrow over a data tile will display the name of the area and a numerical value of said area, depending on which map display is currently active.
  • Changing the map: The grid above the map are buttons that display different maps depending on the selected button. To activate a different map, press one of the buttons above the map and it will display the chosen map.

About the map

Urban Heat Island effect is when an urban area experiences warmer temperatures compares to rural and less-developed urban areas. This effect occurs because the materials used in urban areas such as bricks, concrete and asphalt are dark colours and are efficient at absorbing heat from the sun's energy. This in turn would cause the objects to increase in temperature and when there is a high abundancy of urban structures made of these materials in an area, the collecive temperature of the area would significantly increase because of the higher prescence of urban structures to absorb the energy from the sun.

Because of this Urban Heat Island effect, it is very important to monitor not only the prescence and temperature changes of urban areas, but also the prescence of vegetation. This is because vegetation goes through a process called transpiration where plants absorb water into its roots where the water travels up through the plant and is released through the underside of the leaves through small holes, acting as a natural air conditioner. The presence of vegetation helps reduce the temperature increase from the Urban Heat Island effect because of transpiration which helps with cooling the surrounding area of the vegetation down.

In order to investigate the areas of Greater Melbourne experiencing significant increases in temperature because of the Urban Heat Island effect and the presence of vegetation in said areas, these interactive maps provided will assist with investigating the increase in temperature within Greater Melbourne by Local Government Area (LGA) using government sourced data. Other maps that are also provided are the percentage of tree cover by Local Government Area and by region within Greater Melbourne and maps that illustrate the Heat Vulnerability Index (HVI) of Greater Melbourne within years 2014 and 2018. A Heat Vulnerability Index of 1 indicates the area has low exposure to the sun’s energy, low sensitivity, or higher adaptive capacity to the temperature increase while 5 indicates high exposure, high sensitivity, or low adaptive capacity to the increasing temperature.

Glossary and helpful terms

  • Heat Vulnerability Index(HVI): Numerical identification that indicates which areas are most vulnerable to the increase in heat island effect. It consists of three indicators:
    • Heat Exposure
    • Sensitivity to heat
    • Adaptive capability
  • Vulnerability ratings are determined by the sum of the aggregated indicators and are scaled from 1 to 5:
  • (1 = low vulnerability, 5 = high vulnerability).
  • Urban Heat Island: Urban/metropolitan areas that have a higher above average temperature due to the greater generation, absoprtion and retention of heat due to human activity and man-made structures.
  • Vegetation Cover: Amount of plants and trees that cover the physical surface of the Earth.
  • Urban Tree Cover Percentage: When the prescence of urban green infrastructure such as trees provide natural shade over man-made structures absorb heat, particularly hard and dark-coloured surfaces such as roads and pavement.
